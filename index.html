MODULE 1: Capital and Money Markets

1.1 Structural Framework & Flow of Funds

Financial markets exist to efficiently allocate scarce economic capital from surplus units (household savings, profitable firms) to deficit units (corporations needing expansion capital, governments funding infrastructure).

Essential Transformations Executed by Intermediaries:

  • Maturity Transformation: Converting short-term liabilities (liquid, on-demand bank deposits) into long-term assets (15-to-30-year commercial/residential loans).
  • Risk Transformation (Risk Pooling): Intermediaries exploit the Law of Large Numbers. By aggregating large portfolios of independent loans, they diversify away individual default risks (idiosyncratic risk), creating a lower-risk asset for depositors than any single loan would offer.
  • Liquidity Transformation: Providing savers with highly liquid assets (accessible via ATMs/electronic transfers) while locking funds into highly illiquid long-term corporate assets.
  • Denomination/Volume Transformation: Pooling thousands of small-scale retail deposits into multi-million-dollar corporate credit facilities.

1.2 Money Markets vs. Capital Markets

1. The Money Market: A wholesale, over-the-counter (OTC) market dealing in short-term, highly liquid debt instruments with maturities under one year.

  • Bank Bills (Bill of Exchange): An unconditional order in writing, drawn by one party on another, requiring the bank to pay a fixed sum at a specified future maturity. Highly tradable because the accepting bank guarantees payment.
  • Promissory Notes (Commercial Paper): Unsecured short-term debt instruments issued by highly rated, large corporations to meet immediate liabilities.

2. The Capital Market: Markets facilitating the issuance and trading of long-term financial instruments (maturities exceeding one year).

1.3 Primary vs. Secondary Markets

Primary Market: The market for new securities. Deficit units issue fresh equity or debt instruments to investors to raise new cash capital (e.g., IPOs, rights issues). Cash flows directly from investors to the issuing entity.

Secondary Market: The market where existing securities are bought and sold among investors (e.g., trading existing shares on the ASX secondary platform). No new capital is raised for the issuing firm.

1.4 Regulatory Framework Architecture

Australia uses the "Twin Peaks" regulatory architecture, separating financial system oversight into two primary agencies:

  • Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA): Focuses on the financial soundness, institutional solvency, and systemic stability of deposit-taking institutions, insurance companies, and superannuation funds.
  • Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC): Focuses on market conduct, financial services disclosure, and consumer protection regulation.

MODULE 2: Financial Theories

2.1 The Time Value of Money (TVM)

Compounding: FV = PV × (1 + r)^n

Discounting: PV = FV / (1 + r)^n
Effective Annual Rate (EAR) = [1 + (r_nominal / m)]^m - 1
Ordinary Annuity PV = C × [ (1 - (1 + r)^-n) / r ]

Constant Perpetuity PV = C / r

2.2 Relevant Cash Flow Philosophy

  • Sunk Costs (IGNORE): Past expenditures already incurred that cannot be recovered (e.g., historical feasibility studies).
  • Opportunity Costs (INCLUDE): The net cash forgone by choosing one project over its next best alternative asset deployment.
  • Financing Costs (IGNORE in Cash Flows): Interest expenses and principal repayments must not be deducted from project operational cash flows. Financing costs are captured explicitly within the discount rate (WACC).

2.3 Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)

E(R_i) = R_f + β_i × [E(R_m) - R_f]
  • E(R_i): Required or expected return on asset i.
  • R_f: Risk-free rate of return (long-term government bond yields).
  • β_i (Beta): Measures systematic risk—the sensitivity of the asset's returns relative to the overall market portfolio.
  • E(R_m) - R_f: Market Risk Premium (MRP).

2.4 Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)

Form of Efficiency Information Incorporated into Prices Implication for Analytics
Weak Form All past price and volume historical data. Technical analysis (charting) is useless.
Semi-Strong Form All publicly available information (earnings, news). Fundamental and Technical analysis cannot generate alpha.
Strong Form All information, both public and private/insider. No group can consistently beat the market, even with insider knowledge.

MODULE 3: Risk Identification, Assessment and Management

3.1 Risk vs. Uncertainty

Risk: A decision context where the potential future states of nature are unknown, but the decision-maker can assign objective or subjective mathematical probabilities to those outcomes.

Uncertainty: A decision context where the future outcomes are completely unknown, and it is impossible to assign valid mathematical probabilities due to a total lack of historical precedent.

3.2 Enterprise Financial Risk Inventory

  • Foreign Currency (FX) Risk: Includes Transaction Exposure (contractual settlement risk), Translation Exposure (accounting statement consolidation risk), and Economic Exposure (long-term competitive structural risk).
  • Interest Rate Risk: Mismatch/Pricing Risk and Basis Risk.
  • Liquidity Risk: Funding Liquidity Risk (cash availability) and Asset Liquidity Risk (thin trading markets).
  • Credit Risk: Counterparty defaults on contractual obligations.
  • Sovereign & Political Risk: Expropriation, sudden changes to tax codes, or capital controls blocking fund repatriation.

MODULE 4: Sources and Cost of Finance

4.1 Capital Component Analysis & Costs

Cost of Equity (Gordon Growth): K_e = (D_1 / P_0) + g
After-tax Cost of Debt: After-tax K_d = Pre-tax K_d × (1 - t)
Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC):
WACC = (E/V × K_e) + (P/V × K_p) + (D/V × K_d × (1 - t))

Detailed WACC Worked Example

Scenario:
• Ordinary Shares: 10M outstanding. Market price P_0 = $4.50. Beta (β) = 1.2.
• Debt: 50,000 irredeemable bonds, nominal value $100, trading at $95. Coupon rate = 8%.
• Macro: Risk-free rate (R_f) = 4%, MRP = 6%, Tax (t) = 30%.

Step 1: Calculate Market Values (E and D)
• E = 10,000,000 × $4.50 = $45,000,000
• D = 50,000 × $95 = $4,750,000
• V = $45,000,000 + $4,750,000 = $49,750,000

Step 2: Compute Cost of Equity (K_e) via CAPM
• K_e = 4% + 1.2 × (6%) = 11.2%

Step 3: Compute After-Tax Cost of Debt (K_d)
• Annual Interest (I) = 8% of $100 = $8.
• Pre-tax K_d = $8 / $95 = 8.421%
• After-tax K_d = 8.421% × (1 - 0.30) = 5.895%

Step 4: Synthesize into WACC Formula
• WACC = (($45,000,000 / $49,750,000) × 11.2%) + (($4,750,000 / $49,750,000) × 5.895%)
• WACC = (0.9045 × 11.2%) + (0.0955 × 5.895%) = 10.69%

MODULE 5: Liquidity Management

5.1 The Cash Operating Cycle

Cash Operating Cycle = Inventory Days + Receivables Days - Payables Days

5.2 Working Capital Optimization Models

Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) = Square Root of [ (2 × D × O) / H ]

Balances inventory ordering costs (O) against carrying/holding costs (H) for an annual demand (D).

The Baumol Cash Model: C* = Square Root of [ (2 × T × F) / i ]

Balances fixed transaction costs (F) of liquidating marketable securities against the opportunity cost of interest lost (i) over a total transaction volume (T).

MODULE 6: Capital Structure and Management

6.1 Theoretical Frameworks

  • Modigliani & Miller (M&M) Irrelevance Theorem (No Tax): The total value of a firm is independent of its gearing ratio. The higher cost of equity perfectly offsets the benefit of cheaper debt.
  • M&M with Corporate Tax: Interest payments create a corporate tax shield, increasing levered firm value: V_L = V_U + (t × D).
  • Trade-Off Theory: Real-world balance. Optimal gearing is achieved where the marginal benefit of the tax shield is perfectly offset by the rising present value of financial distress costs (bankruptcy risk, loss of customer trust).
  • Pecking Order Theory: Due to asymmetric information, firms prioritize internal funds (retained earnings) first, straight debt second, and external equity issuance only as a last resort.

MODULE 7: Cash Flow Valuation and Investment

7.1 Appraisal Metrics

Net Present Value (NPV) = Sum of [ CF_t / (1 + k)^t ] - Initial Outlay (CF_0)
Decision Rule: Accept if NPV > 0.
Internal Rate of Return (IRR): The discount rate where NPV = 0.
Decision Rule: Accept if IRR > WACC. Defer to NPV if a conflict occurs on mutually exclusive projects.

7.2 Capital Rationing

Divisible Projects: Rank by Profitability Index (PI) and allocate funds downward until the budget breaks.

Profitability Index (PI) = Present Value of Future Cash Flows / Initial Outlay

Indivisible Projects: Test every feasible combined permutation to select the combination yielding the absolute highest overall NPV.

MODULE 8: Market and Credit Risk Management

8.1 Derivative Instruments Architecture

  • Forward Contracts: Private, over-the-counter (OTC) agreements. High credit default risk and illiquid, but highly customizable.
  • Futures Contracts: Exchange-traded, standardized contracts with daily mark-to-market margins via clearinghouses. Zero counterparty credit risk.
  • Options: Pay an upfront premium for flexibility. Call options provide the right to buy; Put options provide the right to sell at a fixed strike price.

FX Hedging Example (USD $1,000,000 Payable in 90 Days)

• Spot Rate: 1 AUD = 0.7000 USD | 90-day Forward Lock Rate: 1 AUD = 0.6800 USD.
• If the firm leaves it unhedged and the AUD crashes to 0.6200, the cost swells to AUD $1,612,903.
• Utilizing the Forward Contract locks the payment securely at: $1,000,000 / 0.6800 = AUD $1,470,588, removing all exchange volatility.

MODULE 9: Investment Management

9.1 Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)

Portfolio Expected Return: E(R_p) = (w_A × E(R_A)) + (w_B × E(R_B))
Portfolio Variance (2-Asset): σ_p^2 = (w_A^2 × σ_A^2) + (w_B^2 × σ_B^2) + (2 × w_A × w_B × Cov(A,B))

Where Cov(A,B) = ρ_AB × σ_A × σ_B. True risk diversification benefits materialize as long as the correlation coefficient (ρ) drops below +1.0.
